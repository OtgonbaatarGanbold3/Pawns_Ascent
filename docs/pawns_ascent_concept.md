# PAWN'S ASCENT — Concept Document

**PAWN’S ASCENT**
> also known as — The Unranked
Game Concept Document — Story & World Reference
Genre: Roguelike / Board Combat
Tone: Weird / Mythic — Folklore, ancient curses, ambiguous morality
Pillars: Board combat · Roguelike runs · D&D fantasy · Existential horror
# Logline
> A nameless soul arrives in a kingdom where rank is flesh. With no piece to call their own, they fight upward through an endless, shifting plain — only to discover that reaching the throne means becoming the very thing that fed the cycle.
# The World
## The Plain
A vast, featureless expanse that has always existed. No horizon, no sky, no walls. The board tiles beneath the player’s feet shift, crack, and mutate the longer they survive — terrain warping as if the world itself resists being understood.
**ORIGIN MYTH**
No one knows how the Plain came to be. The oldest pieces whisper myths that contradict each other. Some say a god made it. Some say it has always been here. Some say there was once something else — a world before — and the Plain swallowed it. None can be verified. The Plain does not care.
**HOW IT MUTATES**

| Early runs Sparse, navigable. Flat open tiles with predictable enemy placement. The board feels almost safe. |
| --- |


| Mid runs Terrain begins to shift mid-encounter. Cursed tiles appear without warning. Fog creeps from the edges. The logic of the board grows unreliable. |
| --- |


| Late runs Tiles invert. Terrain advantages reverse. The board actively resists the player’s pattern recognition. It does not feel designed. It feels like entropy. |
| --- |

## The Law: Rank is Flesh
In this kingdom, rank is not a title. It is your body. Pawns are small, expendable, limited in movement. Rooks are massive and rigid. Queens are overwhelming and terrifying in scale. This is not metaphor. This is not symbolic. No one voted for this. No one alive remembers a world before it.
The oldest pieces simply say: “It has always been so.” Contradictory myths fill the gaps, and none are provably true. The law exists. It is obeyed not because it is enforced — but because it is reality.
**THE HIERARCHY**

| Chess piece | D&D / fantasy form | Special power |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Pawn | Villager → Warrior | Evolves on 3 kills — the only piece that can |
| Knight | Ranger / Berserker | L-shaped leap — inflicts bleed on landing |
| Bishop | Cleric / Dark Sorcerer | Diagonal movement — curses tiles it crosses |
| Rook | Siege Golem / Paladin | Line smash — knockback on capture |
| Queen | War Goddess / Archmage | Omnidirectional — AoE burst on kill |
| King | Overlord / Lich King | Survives first kill — passive dread aura |

# The Player Character
## The Unranked
Every soul that arrives on the Plain is assigned a rank on contact. The assignment happens automatically, immediately — a function of the world’s law that no piece fully understands. The player arrived with no assignment. The Plain doesn’t know what to do with them.
By default, the Plain gave them the lowest available shape: a Pawn. But the law doesn’t quite apply to the Unranked the same way it applies to everyone else. Other pieces are fixed in their form. The Unranked is not. This is why they can evolve. This is why others fear them, even when they shouldn’t.

| Starting state Pawn form. No name. No memory of before death. One step at a time. Slow. Easily overlooked. The Unranked carries no personal history — only forward motion. | Why they fight At first: survival. Then: curiosity — what is above? Then: dread — does above even end? The motivation shifts across runs as more of the world’s lore is absorbed. |
| --- | --- |

**KEY DISTINCTION**

| Why the Unranked can evolve Ranked pieces are their rank. It is written into their body by the law. The Unranked was never written — so the law’s ink has not dried. Each kill, each item, each level is the Unranked writing their own rank in real time. The older pieces find this deeply wrong. A few find it beautiful. |
| --- |

# Enemies
## Desperate pieces, not monsters
Most pieces fight the Unranked not from hatred but from obligation. The law demands that lower ranks yield to higher — and an unranked piece is an anomaly that threatens the entire order. A pawn who fights the player is protecting their position. A knight that charges is following old instinct carved into its body. The tragedy is that most of them know it’s senseless.
A few will say so before they attack.
**ENEMY VOICE — TONE REFERENCE**
> “You shouldn’t be here.”
> “I don’t enjoy this either.”
> “The plain keeps score. Not me.”
> “Don’t come back. You always come back.”
**ENEMY DESIGN PRINCIPLE**

| The rule of ambivalence No enemy is purely evil. Every piece is a dead soul wearing a body the law assigned them. Higher-ranked pieces are not more villainous — they are more trapped. A Queen has enormous power and no more freedom than a Pawn. The King has been king for longer than anyone can remember. It is not pleased about this. |
| --- |

**ENEMY CATEGORIES**

| Common pieces (Pawns, Knights) Fight from fear and obligation. Short, bleak pre-fight lines. May reference past lives vaguely. Occasionally offer warning instead of aggression. | Mid-rank pieces (Bishops, Rooks) More resigned than afraid. Have been on the Plain longer. Speak with dark authority. Their dialogue hints at lore the player cannot yet verify. |
| --- | --- |


| High-rank pieces (Queens) Rare, formidable, contemptuous but not cruel. Queens have seen unranked challengers before. They know how it ends. They attack anyway — because the law demands it. | The King (final boss) Not a monster. A sovereign that has survived every cycle and is exhausted by it. The confrontation with the King is more philosophical than combative — at first. |
| --- | --- |

# Run Structure
## The loop
Each run is a single attempt to climb as high as possible across the shifting Plain before death resets the board. Runs are self-contained but not isolated — meta-progression carries lore fragments, unlocked piece forms, and item memory across deaths.
**RUN PHASES**

| 1. Arrival The Unranked appears on the Plain with no items, Pawn form, no memory of the previous run. The board is sparsely populated. Terrain is simple. |
| --- |


| 2. Early encounters Groups of Pawns and Knights. Terrain is straightforward with minor advantages (elevation tiles, fog patches). After each encounter the player is offered a draft: choose 1 of 3 items or abilities. |
| --- |


| 3. Mid-run escalation Bishop and Rook encounters. Terrain mutates more aggressively. Items from earlier begin to synergize. The board’s logic grows less reliable. Some enemies begin offering deals instead of combat. |
| --- |


| 4. Late-run dread Queen-tier encounters. The Plain is actively hostile. The player’s power is significant but the board is almost unrecognizable from what it was at the start. Some pieces refuse to fight — and simply watch. |
| --- |


| 5. The King A single, fixed encounter at the furthest reachable point. The board goes still. The King speaks before it fights. Defeating it ends the run — but not in the way the player expects. |
| --- |

# The Ending — The Cycle
## Defeating the King
When the King falls, the Plain pauses. No fanfare. No music swell. The board simply stops.
The Unranked’s form changes. They grow. The law, which had never fully applied to them, applies now — in full. They are assigned. They are crowned. They are the King.
A new run begins. The player is the King now. The board is laid out beneath them. Other unranked souls will come. The cycle continues.
**WHAT THE KING SAYS**
> “Do you understand what you are doing?”
> “I did too. I was certain I was different.”
> “The plain has no edge. I have looked.”
> “Come then. Add another ring to the count.”
**WHAT THE ENDING MEANS MECHANICALLY**

| The meta-loop Each cycle, the player retains fragments: a piece of lore, a whisper from the previous King, a tile that looks slightly different. The full picture of the world’s origin assembles across multiple runs — not within a single one. Completing the cycle several times reveals contradictory truths about the Plain’s origin. None resolve cleanly. This is intentional. |
| --- |


| The credits The credits roll over the new board being laid out beneath the crowned Unranked. No escape screen. No congratulations message. Just the Plain, continuing. |
| --- |

# Tone & Writing Guide
## How this world communicates
Pawn’s Ascent tells its story sideways. There are no cutscenes, no exposition dumps. The world’s lore lives in enemy dialogue, item names and descriptions, and the visual mutation of the terrain. Players who pay attention will piece together more than players who don’t — but neither will get the complete picture.
**REFERENCE POINTS**

| Gameplay feel Balatro’s compulsive one-more-run loop. Tight, satisfying turns. A growing sense of power that curdles into unease the closer you get to winning. | Narrative feel Ursula K. Le Guin crossed with Hades. A world with internal logic, told sideways — through enemy lines and item descriptions, not narration. |
| --- | --- |

**WRITING RULES**
- No exposition dumps. The Plain’s history is never explained in full.
- Enemy dialogue is short, bleak, occasionally dark-wry. Maximum 2–3 lines before a fight.
- Item descriptions are written as if authored by a piece who once owned the item.
- Silence is intentional. Empty spaces in dialogue mean more than filled ones.
- Contradictions are features. Two myths can both be true, or neither, or it doesn’t matter.
- The world never winks at the player. It takes itself seriously even at its strangest.
**WHAT THIS SHOULD NEVER BE**
- Ironic or self-aware — this is not a parody of chess or fantasy.
- Explained — the mystery of the Plain’s origin is permanent, not a twist to be revealed.
- Hopeful — the tone is existential, not nihilistic, but it offers no comfort.
- Villain-driven — there is no antagonist. The system is the horror.
# Design Principles
## How story serves mechanics

| The board as the world The randomly generated maps are not levels — they are the Plain’s current mood. Terrain mutations should feel like the world’s unconscious resistance, not game design difficulty spikes. Cursed tiles, fog, and inverted advantages are the Plain thinking. |
| --- |


| Items as relics Items found after encounters should feel like things other pieces left behind. Not power-ups — remnants. Each item description is a fragment of someone else’s story on the Plain. Stat effects are secondary to the story they carry. |
| --- |


| Evolution as transformation When the Unranked evolves from Pawn to Knight to Bishop and beyond, this should feel like a change of body, not a level-up screen. The player is writing their rank in real time. The transformation should be uncanny, not triumphant. |
| --- |


| The roguelike loop as the myth Each run is not a retry — it is another soul cycling through the Plain’s logic. The player is not the same Unranked every time. Or perhaps they are. The Plain does not distinguish. This ambiguity is the point. |
